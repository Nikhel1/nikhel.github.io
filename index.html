Research

 

In my master thesis, I worked on modeling techniques to forecast cosmological parameter constraints for the upcoming telescope Cerro Chajnantor Atacama Telescope (CCAT). I developed some well tested libraries for halo mass function, clustering of clusters, MCMC and Fisher analysis. My estimates show that the cluster count and the angular clustering experiments from CCAT will constrain the dark energy equation of state with ~ 6% accuracy and that the wider surveys constrain cosmology more tightly than the deeper ones. My plan is to publish this work once survey parameters are finalized within the collaboration. The current draft version is available here (Gupta et al. in prep.).

In my PhD, I continued working on galaxy clusters both in the framework of observations and hydrodynamical simulations. Below are brief descriptions of the projects that I have been leading.

 

1. “High Frequency Cluster Radio Galaxies: Luminosity Functions and Implications for SZE Selected Cluster Samples” (Gupta et al. 2016): I studied the overdensity of radio point sources in the direction of X-ray-selected galaxy clusters from the Meta-Catalog of X-ray detected Clusters of galaxies (MCXC) at South Pole Telescope (SPT) and Sydney University Molonglo Sky Survey (SUMSS) frequencies. The main motivation of this project was to quantify the incompleteness in the SPT galaxy cluster sample due to the flux of point sources residing in the galaxy clusters. The study is unique. This is the first time that the cluster radio galaxy luminosity functions have been constructed at the detection frequencies of SPT clusters i.e 95 and 150 GHz frequencies. Results show that the impact of radio galaxies on the Sunyaev- Zel’dovich effect selection with SPT is modest, but more information is needed to understand the impact of the redshift evolution of the luminosity function on the SZE selection.

 

2. “SZE Observables, Pressure Profiles and Center Offsets in Magneticum Simulation Galaxy Clusters” (Gupta et al. 2016): I studied the galaxy cluster thermal Sunyaev-Zel’dovich effect (SZE) signal Y and pressure profiles using Magneticum Pathfinder hydrodynamical simulations. I presented a new generalized NFW model that follows the trends in pressure profiles with cluster mass and redshift, and I showed that hydrostatic mass estimates would be biased at the 20 percent level. Uncorrelated large scale structure along the line of sight leads to an increase in the SZE signal and scatter that is more pronounced for low mass clusters, resulting in non self-similar trends in both mass and redshift and a mass dependent scatter that is ∼16% at low masses. I also presented a model of the offsets between the center of the gravitational potential and the SZE center that follows the variations with cluster mass and redshift. The results from this study are interesting from the perspective of understanding galaxy clusters and their evolution with mass and redshift as physical systems. In addition, the results are crucial for accurate modeling of the mass-observable relation within cosmological analyses of SZE selected clusters samples like those from SPT and in the use of weak lensing to accurately calibrate the SZE observable-mass relations.

 

3. I am currently working on the continuation of the first project on high frequency radio galaxies using Dark Energy Survey (DES) and SPT datasets to study the high frequency radio galaxy luminosity functions (LFs) in clusters and the field. This cluster sample is much larger and extends to much higher redshifts (z~0.8) as compared to the MCXC sample we initially examined. Outcomes of this study will be an improved understanding of high frequency radio galaxies, including differences between those systems in clusters and the field and how these populations change as a function of redshift. In addition, the DES counterparts of the high- frequency radio galaxies will allow us to understand the hosts of these systems, measuring their redshifts and stellar masses (in many cases). This information then allows direct constraints on the radio galaxy luminosity function and its evolution. Moreover, through the clustering study (within photo-z bins) we will be able to constrain the characteristic halo masses of the radio galaxy hosts.

 

4. I am currently studying the polarisation properties of extragalactic radio sources. The polarisation of the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) has a wealth of potential information that can be exploited to probe the early Universe. The linear polarisation of the CMB can be decomposed into E-mode and B-mode components. The E-modes are mainly sourced by scalar perturbations and the B-modes by the tensor perturbations and the distortion of E-modes through gravitational lensing by the intervening matter. The B-modes induced by gravitational lensing are potential contaminants towards the detection of primordial B-modes generated by tensor perturbations or inflationary gravitational waves (IGW). The lensing induced B-mode signal is interesting on its own as it can be used to reconstruct the projected gravitational potential between the observer and the last scattering surface. A large contribution to this lensing signal comes from the polarised emissions from extragalactic radio sources and galactic dust. Currently, the polarisation properties of extragalactic radio sources are poorly constrained. In this talk, I will present our ongoing effort to measure the polarisation fraction for the extragalactic radio sources detected in 500 square degree of the southern sky observed with SPTpol, a dual-frequency polarisation-sensitive camera that was deployed on the 10-meter South Pole Telescope.
